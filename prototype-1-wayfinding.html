Wayfinding design is essential to the interaction of people and cities, and augmented reality (AR) is newly emerging for consumers as a form of human-computer interaction. This module will explore what potential there might be to innovate wayfinding practices with AR.

How might we use augmented reality to improve wayfinding in contexts where good wayfinding might not exist as part of the existing environment?

design question

In Wayfinding and Augmented Reality, I outlined best practices in both the wayfinding and augmented reality (AR) fields. My findings led me to the design question above.

scope

I started by brainstorming opportunities for wayfinding augmentation.

Where?

  • Hopsitals
  • Malls
  • Campuses
  • Suburbs

Who?

  • Children
  • Seniors
  • Tourists
  • Disabled

I decided to narrow the scope of the problem down to the context of campuses in order to be able to design something that I could understand and test with my subjects in arms reach.

Students

  • Unfamiliar part of campus
  • New to the university
  • Visiting/prospective
  • Newly constructed areas

Visitors

  • Tours
  • Research
  • Meetings/events
  • Wandering

Since AR on mobile phones is still an emerging technology, I leaned towards designing an add-on to an existing app, instead of a standalone AR app. My hypothesis here is that people are more likely to try AR wayfinding in an app they already trust, than having to go through the work to download a new app.

research

Aside from the state-of-the art and literature I read up on in my learning module, I wanted to learn specifically, about the kinds of frustrations people might have navigating a campus. So, I asked a few folks, and formed personas around the types of frustrations and questions potential users might have.

Students

  • Difficult to orient self on campus
  • May only have classes on one part of the campus
  • Similar looking buildings and spaces

Visitors

  • Signage is hard to find
  • No maps on campus
  • Building names are confusing

workflows

I started thinking about the workflow in the context of someone walking around on campus and the steps that they might have when trying to find a building they're supposed to get to.

The workflow has a few points of entry. Ideally the AR feature should be discoverable, but it also might only make sense to notify users who are on campus for the first few times.

Using the principles of wayfinding, as outlined in Wayfinding and Augmented Reality, the interface should augment the principles that the existing built environment might not already feature—transforming poor wayfinding environments into more optimal ones.

de-"sign" system

Since one of the wayfinding problems people defined was the lack of, or hidden nature of signage on campus, I decided to use AR to provide the clear and salient signage that was missing.

For each type of sign typically used in wayfinding design, I designed a digital equivalent.

navigational experience

By fitting the AR experience on top of Google Maps existing experience, I was able to take advantage of Google's existing design and interaction patterns from Material Design, as well as provide a trustworthy experience for wayfinding.

augmentation

One of the challenges I encountered in creating an informative augmented experience was that of optimizing information density and avoiding complete obstruction of reality.

In addition to the 4 major types of signage (plus instructional signage), I overlaid navigational information like the path to follow and the estimated time to arrival. Additionally, secondary signage provides context of proximal landmarks so users can familiarize themselves with their surroundings. Secondary signage aims to provide a teaching/learning experience along with a goal-oriented navigational experience.

wandering vs. navigating

I focused on a workflow for people with a destination in mind, but ideally, an additional workflow should be considered, allowing users to wander and learn their surroundings on their own terms.
